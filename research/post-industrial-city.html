Post-Industrial City

Research on Urban Transformation in Asia

"The post-industrial city is not a problem to be solved, but a condition to be engaged — a territory of opportunity within the ruins of the industrial age."

— Albert Pope, Rice University

The rapid industrialization of Asian cities over the past half-century has produced urban landscapes of extraordinary complexity and contradiction. Cities like Beijing, Shanghai, Seoul, and Incheon have undergone transformations of a scale and speed unprecedented in history — moving within a single generation from agricultural economies to post-industrial knowledge centers.

This research examines the specific urban conditions produced by this transformation: the abandoned industrial zones, the informal settlements, the super-blocks of planned housing, the new transit corridors, and the hybrid landscapes where traditional patterns of urban life persist alongside global economic flows. These conditions are not simply problems to be remediated, but territories rich with possibility — places where new forms of urban life are being invented in real time.

The Now Institute's Post-Industrial City research focuses on three Asian urban contexts: the established industrial peripheries of Beijing and Seoul; the rapidly growing tier-2 and tier-3 cities of inland China; and the emerging urban corridors connecting major metropolitan regions. Each context presents distinct challenges and opportunities for urban research and design intervention.

Working with partner universities including Inha University (Korea) and Beijing University of Technology (China), as well as the full network of Now Institute research partners, this research combines on-the-ground fieldwork with comparative urban analysis and design speculation. The goal is to develop frameworks for understanding and engaging post-industrial urban conditions that can inform both academic research and professional practice.

The research asks fundamental questions about the future of Asian cities: How do industrial landscapes transform into post-industrial ones? What role can design play in shaping this transformation? How can the informal economies and social patterns that have thrived in industrial zones persist and evolve in a post-industrial context? What new institutions, infrastructures, and public spaces are needed?

These questions are addressed through a combination of historical research, comparative case studies, mapping and data analysis, design proposals, and direct engagement with local communities, governments, and organizations working in the field. The results are intended to contribute both to scholarly discourse and to practical decision-making by the actors shaping these rapidly changing cities.
