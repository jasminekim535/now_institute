Haiti Now

How can culture become a catalyst for recovery?

How do we build resilience from the ground up?

What does reconstruction mean beyond physical infrastructure?

Haiti Now is an initiative of The Now Institute examining the intersection of cultural resilience, urban recovery, and community-driven reconstruction in post-earthquake Haiti.

Post-Disaster Haiti

On January 12, 2010, a 7.0 magnitude earthquake struck Haiti, devastating the capital Port-au-Prince and surrounding areas. The disaster killed over 200,000 people, displaced 1.5 million, and destroyed much of the country's already fragile infrastructure. The response from the international community was swift and massive, yet the complexity of rebuilding a nation already burdened by extreme poverty, political instability, and environmental vulnerability has proven immense.

The Now Institute engaged with Haiti's reconstruction through the lens of culture — examining how cultural institutions, practices, and identity can serve as foundations for long-term urban and social recovery. This work has been conducted in partnership with local organizations, international NGOs, and governmental bodies, bringing together research from architecture, urban planning, anthropology, and policy.

Relief, Recovery, and Planning

The distinction between relief, recovery, and long-term planning is critical in post-disaster contexts. Relief addresses immediate needs — shelter, water, food, medical care. Recovery extends over months and years, rebuilding housing, infrastructure, and livelihoods. Planning looks further, envisioning the kind of city and society that might emerge from crisis.

Too often in disaster recovery, the urgency of relief crowds out the necessary work of planning. The Now Institute's approach insists that planning must begin simultaneously with relief — that the decisions made in the first weeks and months after a disaster shape decades of urban development. In Haiti, this meant engaging immediately with questions of land tenure, informal settlement patterns, cultural geography, and long-term economic development.

Cultural Reconstruction

Physical reconstruction — rebuilding buildings and infrastructure — is necessary but insufficient. Cultural reconstruction addresses the social fabric, the practices, institutions, and spaces that give communities meaning and coherence. In Haiti, the earthquake destroyed not just buildings but many of the cultural institutions that anchored community life: schools, churches, markets, community centers, and gathering places.

The Now Institute's research examined how cultural institutions might be rebuilt not simply as they were, but as catalysts for broader urban and social transformation. This involved studying existing informal cultural practices, mapping networks of community organization, and proposing new typologies for cultural infrastructure adapted to Haitian conditions and needs.

Cultural Opportunity

Crisis creates opportunity. The destruction of existing built fabric, while devastating, also opens possibilities for reimagining the city. In Port-au-Prince, the earthquake cleared land, disrupted entrenched patterns of land ownership and use, and created conditions in which new forms of urban organization became possible.

The Now Institute identified specific sites and programs where cultural investment could leverage broader urban transformation — where the strategic placement of cultural infrastructure could catalyze economic development, strengthen community networks, and create new patterns of public life. These proposals were developed in dialogue with community organizations, local government, and international partners.

Cultural Resilience

Resilience is the capacity of a system to absorb disturbance and reorganize while undergoing change. Cultural resilience refers specifically to the ability of communities to maintain their identity, values, and social bonds through crisis and change. Haiti's cultural traditions — including Vodou, carnival, music, and storytelling — have historically served as profound sources of community cohesion and resilience.

The Now Institute's work on cultural resilience asks how urban design and planning can support and strengthen these traditions, creating spaces and infrastructure that reinforce community bonds rather than disrupting them. This means working with existing cultural practices rather than imposing foreign models, and designing institutions that can serve multiple roles and adapt to changing needs over time.
