L.A. Now

What is a city? The experience, memory, parameters, systems and personality of Los Angeles are collapsed in 4 volumes of conceptual and graphic almanacs and research proposals.

Volume 1

In 2002, the L.A. Now series was launched as a collaboration between the NOW Institute, UCLA, and RAND to define Los Angeles as both a city and a system. The project combined data analysis with design research to explore how the city functions and how it might evolve.

Across four volumes developed over four years, L.A. Now translated large-scale urban data into visual and spatial analysis, examining the region's structure, challenges, and identity. The work received wide recognition and was awarded the Progressive Architecture Award in 2006.

Volume 1 presents a comprehensive visual atlas of Los Angeles at the start of the 21st century. It organizes complex urban data into four key categories: the city as a whole, its habitats, its people, and its economy. The result is a portrait of Los Angeles as fragmented, heterogeneous, and constantly evolving.

At the core of the study is a central challenge: Los Angeles continues to grow rapidly while its infrastructure remains under strain. With millions of additional residents projected, the city must rethink its planning strategies, particularly the role of downtown as both a symbolic and functional center that can reconnect its dispersed urban regions.

The project frames downtown Los Angeles as a potential "connective tissue" for the wider metropolis, where design, data, and urban planning converge to support future growth and integration.

Volume 2

From the research and analysis compiled in the first step, LA Now Volume 2 was a proposal for interpretive strategies that would accommodate the city's fragmentation, heterogeneity, emergent orders and non-linearity. The resulting projects have optimistic and ambitious aspirations, but they are not utopian in ideology. Operating with found logics, they engage tactics that promote fluidity, flexibility, and interaction of economic, social, and financial forces.

Volume 3+4

The research and speculations from Volume 1 & 2 were expanded in LA Now Volume 3+4 with a deliberate focus on revitalizing the Arts District, Little Tokyo and the LA River in downtown Los Angeles. As the last of the series, this volume continues explorations on Los Angeles' future, focusing on the Chavez Ravine. Centrally located and gifted with commanding high view of the city, the project proposes the relocation of Dodger Stadium to downtown and the infusion of a 30,000 resident community on the site that integrates and expands Elysian Park's potential.

L.A. Now: Volume 3 develops a set of housing proposals grounded in research and analysis, focused on introducing residential density into the downtown Los Angeles core.

The site is located in the Arts District, bounded by the 101 Freeway, the Los Angeles River, Alameda Street, and 4th Street. It sits at a key threshold between downtown Los Angeles and East Los Angeles, acting as a mediator between two distinct urban conditions.

In response to growing population pressure and increasing demand for central housing, the volume examines the feasibility of expanding residential development to the edges of the downtown core. The proposals leverage the site's proximity to culturally active neighborhoods, including the Southern California Institute of Architecture and planned transit expansion through the Gold Line connection from Pasadena.

Although the Arts District currently has a fragmented and underdefined identity, the research positions it as a strategic opportunity for transformation—capable of evolving into a major residential and cultural anchor within downtown Los Angeles.

L.A. Now: Volume 4 continues the series' speculation on the future of Los Angeles, extending the conceptual framework developed in the earlier volumes while shifting focus to Chavez Ravine as a new geographic and cultural node within the metropolitan region.

The volume proposes the relocation of Los Angeles Dodgers Stadium to downtown Los Angeles, alongside the development of a new residential district within Elysian Park. This dual proposal repositions Chavez Ravine as a site of both infrastructure and housing, reactivating its historical identity as a former residential landscape.

The project explores how high-intensity programs such as a stadium interact with surrounding urban systems, particularly in terms of density, access, and land use. It reframes the relationship between program and site as a productive tension that can generate new forms of urban structure.

At its core, Volume 4 investigates how infrastructure—ecological, cultural, and transportation networks—can be integrated into large-scale housing and stadium development. The result is a speculative model for layering nature, public space, and urban density within a single continuous system.
